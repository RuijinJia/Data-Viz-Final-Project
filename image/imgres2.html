Air Pollution Tied to 30,000 U.S. Deaths

Even counties that met national standards were adversely affected by fine particulate matter, a new study finds.

By Gaby Galvin, Staff Writer July 24, 2019
By Gaby Galvin, Staff Writer July 24, 2019, at 12:05 p.m.
More

Air Pollution and Life Expectancy

More

A new study suggests more could be done to reduce the harms of air pollution.(Pete Saloutos/Getty Images)

Air pollution has contributed to tens of thousands of deaths in the U.S. – even in counties that meet national air quality standards, a new study suggests.

Between 1999 and 2015, exposure to fine particulate matter – tiny, inhalable particles that can originate from cars, power plants and other sources – was linked to an estimated 30,369 deaths in the contiguous U.S., according to an analysis published this week in the journal PLOS Medicine. Those deaths reduced national life expectancy by an estimated 0.15 years for women and 0.13 years for men.

These effects were seen across the U.S., but affected some counties more sharply than others. Declines in life expectancy tied to air pollution were even greater in counties with lower income levels and higher poverty rates, as well as those with a smaller share of high school graduates and a greater proportion of black residents, the study found.

"Current (fine particulate matter) pollution continues to contribute to mortality and loss of longevity in the USA, with larger harms in poorer counties," researchers said. "Further lowering (this type of) pollution is likely to benefit the health of the entire U.S. population and lower health inequalities."

States With Best Natural Environment

Some of the disparities may be explained by other issues that contribute to lower life expectancies for certain populations, the analysis says. It's difficult to establish a direct causal relationship in observational studies, but researchers controlled for factors like education, unemployment, poverty and smoking rates to ensure their estimates were sound across more than 3,000 counties in every state but Alaska and Hawaii.

"Doing everything that you can reasonably do to rule out other explanations, you still ended up with a few tens of thousands of deaths," Majid Ezzati, lead author of the study and a professor of global environmental health at Imperial College London, told CNN.

The type of particles measured in the study – PM2.5 – are so small they can reach the bloodstream, and can hurt the lungs and heart. Fine particulate matter also can make the air appear hazy and damage the environment.

Air pollution levels are currently at "relatively low levels," the study noted, with most parts of the country meeting national air quality standards, due in large part to clean air policies and technology advancements that have reduced pollution.

The national annual standard for fine particulate matter is 12 micrograms per cubic meter of air (ug/m3), with average annual concentrations ranging from 2.8 ug/m3 in Apache County, Arizona, to 13.2 ug/m3 in Tulare County, California, in 2015.

Still, about 9% of the U.S. population lived in counties with pollution levels above World Health Organization air quality guidelines that year, researchers said.

Between 1999 and 2015, "pollution reduction was largest in the most polluted areas, such as southern California and some southern states" like Alabama and Georgia, the study found, but pollution also had the greatest effect on mortality and life expectancy in these areas.

The findings suggest stricter air quality regulations could further reduce deaths and improve longevity, but researchers noted "policy resistance" to such measures.

"If we make the current standards more stringent," Ezzati told CNN, "there is a high likelihood we will delay tens of thousands of deaths … and people will live longer as a result of it."
